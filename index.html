Hello, howdy, hi! I'm an Astronomy Ph.D. Candidate at the University of Maryland, College Park studying how stars and planets evolve together. Mainly, magnetic activity on small young stars and how it evolves over their lifetimes using space-based, high cadence photometry from the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS). I use both machine learning and numerical techniques to charcterize flares in the ongoing 7+ years from this all-sky survey. My research uses a data-driven approach to provide insight into how stellar activity affects atmospheric conditions and the observability of developing planets. Of particular interest is bridging the gap between observable flare emission and how current uncertainties can affect predictions of exoplanet atmospheric evolution.

In addition to this work, I have a passion for making sure my research makes it back into the community through outreach and education. From my experience, the logistics and culture of academia can often limit their extent of implementation within their local communities. A decent amount of this stems from the disconnect between a competitive research environment and how the public interfaces with the science done there. Understanding how people learn and dismantling unnecessary barriers to effective communication are key drivers in the course of my career. That requires being familiar with the mechanisms that prevent people from receiving the support that they need and staying aware of developing pedagogy to be a fruitful mentor and advocate. I've put this action by participating in formal programs such as Terrapin Teachers and fostering learning communities as a NASA SCoPE Affiliate.

✶· ─ · ·Research Interests· · ─ ·✶

My work focuses on reducing large data sets. Whether for observational predictions or fundamental properties, the vast amount of observational data available from survey missions requires efficiency with limited people power. I focus on blending canonical and machine learning methods to ensure the interpretability of astrophysical data. As statistical algorithms continue to advance by leaps and bounds, I aim to integrate these tools sustainably to address some of our most pressing questions.

Currently, I study how stars and planets evolve together. Outside broader luminosity changes as stars age, magnetic activity is one of the largest sources of stellar variability. As stars decrease in mass, a larger fraction of their interior becomes convective, leading to strong magnetic dynamos. This translates to large star spots and frequent flaring, which can produce immense amounts of panchromatic energy beyond expected for that star’s temperature. Unfortunately for rocky planets, they have their highest occurrence rate around these low-mass stars. If we’re talking about keeping an atmosphere on these planets, the outlook’s not great. This phenomenon, coined as the Cosmic Shoreline, informs the relationship between stellar behavior, planetary properties, and atmospheric retention. What does a planet need to withstand its star? What kind of star allows for rocky exoplanet atmospheres? Observations of these planetary systems with telescopes like JWST and Hubble have provided tremendous insight into atmospheres beyond our Solar System. However, these resources are in high demand with very limited allocation time. It is imperative that we are able to both accurately predict and interpret these observations.

My piece of this puzzle is the stellar side. I use optical lightcurves from the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite to characterize magnetic activity for low-mass stars and how it evolves. Given the high cadence and long baseline nature of the data, flares are abundant. Their characteristic shape of a steep rise and slow decline allows for study into their energy contribution to the local radiation environment. By adding flaring activity to the expected temperature evolution of the star, we can present a more realistic picture of the environment these planets are developing in. By measuring stars at all ages, we can trace how the intensity of magentic activity evolves and apply this to modelled planets' atmospheres. While the optical is the most accessible for observing flares, the UV is the main operative wavelength band that drives atmospheric evolution through processes like water dissociation. Flare-focused surveys in higher energy band are sparse with translations from the NUV/FUV to the optical having large error bars. Any precursor science to study exoplanets has to take into consideration the behavior of the host star.

If we don’t have reliable estimates of the true UV flux, how reliable are the limits we can place on a planet’s evolution? Which observations can be made ahead or concurrently to better interpret the data? Can we match the observed characteristics of these planets with our current suite of atmospheric models? These are some of the questions I want to help answer.
